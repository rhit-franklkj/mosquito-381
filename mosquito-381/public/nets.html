Mosquito Nets

Consequences

   This was the method that was the least forthcoming with potential issues. Nets are often treated with insecticides, which may pose ecological challenges similar to those in the next method.

   However, since this specification does not mention insecticide treatment, the nets will be analyzed under the assumption that this is not a factor.

   Another consideration is that individuals are responsible for hanging these nets. If some fail to do so or install them incorrectly, pathogens may persist in the environment.

   Additional funds may be required for education on how the nets work and why they should be hung up in the first place, which should be considered as an additional cost beyond the nets themselves.

   Another potential issue could be mosquito bites acquired during daytime hours, which may result in the pathogens remaining.

   If every infected person were covered by a net, preventing mosquitoes from biting them and spreading the pathogen, transmission would cease to be a concern.

   Mosquitoes as a group would still exist, but the primary concern is their role as pathogen carriers. If any of these pathogens can infect other organisms, this strategy would need to be permanent, requiring periodic net replacements.

Costs

   Each net costs approximately $2, so purchasing 0.871 billion nets would total around $1.742 billion.

   Remarkably, this method is far cheaper than using nuclear weapons and comes with significantly fewer risks.

   Some additional funding may be required for education, but considering the alternative would cost trillions, a small additional expense per net remains far more affordable.
